Squarespace vs Custom Website Cost (2026): Real Numbers

April 23, 2026 · 8 min read

Squarespace looks cheaper on day one. By year three, a custom website is often the cheaper option — and it was always the better one.

Most small business owners compare Squarespace and a custom website on the wrong axis: month-one price. Squarespace is $23 per month, a custom site is $1,500 up front. Easy math, Squarespace wins.

But month-one price is the least useful comparison in the decision. The real question is what each one costs you — in dollars and in missed opportunities — over three to five years of actually running a business on it.

Here's the real breakdown.

What a Squarespace site actually costs in year one

The advertised price and the real price are different. Squarespace's core plans as of 2026:

  • Personal — $16/month ($192/year). No advanced analytics, no custom code, no selling.
  • Business — $23/month ($276/year). Sell products, custom CSS, basic stats. This is the realistic minimum for a business.
  • Commerce Basic — $27/month ($324/year). For stores doing $1k+/month online.
  • Commerce Advanced — $49/month ($588/year). Abandoned cart recovery, subscriptions, gift cards.

But the plan fee is only the start. In year one you'll also pay:

  • Domain renewal — $20–$25/year after the free first-year domain expires.
  • Email hosting — about $6/user/month through Google Workspace (Squarespace stopped bundling this years ago). Two mailboxes: $144/year.
  • Premium template or third-party add-ons — $50–$200/year if you want booking, calculators, real SEO plugins, or anything beyond stock blocks.
  • A Squarespace designer if you want meaningful customization — $75–$150/hour, 5–15 hours for a small site. Call it $500–$2,000 one-time.
Realistic year-one Squarespace total: $450–$900 if you DIY, $1,200–$3,000 if you hire a Squarespace designer.

So "Squarespace is $16/month" is technically true and practically misleading. A real small business ends up spending $500–$900 in year one even with a scrappy DIY approach.

What a custom website actually costs in year one

A custom website is a one-time build plus near-zero ongoing cost. At Sitebolt we charge a flat $1,500 for the build. Here's the real year-one number:

  • Build — $1,500 one-time.
  • Domain — $12–$20/year (most registrars).
  • Hosting — $0 on Cloudflare Pages or Workers free tier for the vast majority of small business sites. If you exceed free tier, $5/month.
  • Email hosting — same $6/user/month through Google Workspace. Two mailboxes: $144/year.

Year-one custom total: $1,656–$1,724. Roughly 2–3× the price of Squarespace.

But year two is where things flip.

The indirect costs Squarespace users don't see

Dollars are the easy part. The costs that don't show up on a line item are usually bigger.

Speed and SEO

Squarespace sites typically score 50–75 on Google PageSpeed mobile. A well-built custom site on Cloudflare regularly hits 90+. Google uses page speed as a ranking factor, and every additional second of load time costs roughly 7% of your conversions. A custom site that loads in 0.8 seconds converts better than a Squarespace site that loads in 2.4 — and it ranks higher, too.

Design constraints

Squarespace templates have rails. You can customize within them, but you can't easily have a custom homepage layout, a bespoke calculator, or a unique on-brand interaction without writing custom code — and custom code inside Squarespace is brittle, hard to maintain, and partially undone every time they update the template engine.

Lock-in

You cannot export a working Squarespace site. Content yes, the site no. If you decide to leave after three years, you're rebuilding from scratch. The three years of monthly fees bought you nothing transferable.

Owner edits vs developer edits

This one cuts the other way. Squarespace gives you drag-and-drop editing; a custom site often doesn't, unless it's built on a CMS like WordPress, Ghost, or a headless setup. If you're a business owner who updates content weekly, this trade matters. If you update every few months, it barely does.

When Squarespace is the right choice

Squarespace makes sense if several of these are true for you:

  • You plan to edit the site yourself weekly or more.
  • Your budget is truly under $500 and will be for the next 12 months.
  • You need to sell products today and don't want to deal with Stripe + a custom store.
  • You're fine with a site that looks like the template most of your competitors also use.
  • You're launching something experimental you're unsure will survive a year.

For a side project or a test, Squarespace is fine. Nobody's judging.

When a custom site is the right choice

Custom is the right call when:

  • This is your real business and you expect to operate it for 3+ years.
  • You want the site to be measurably faster than competitors'.
  • You want a design that looks like yours, not like template #47.
  • You want SEO that actually compounds — real schema, clean markup, fast hosting.
  • You don't want to pay monthly forever.
  • You don't edit your site yourself weekly anyway.

The last point is the quiet one: most small business owners don't actually use drag-and-drop editors. They update a page maybe four times a year. For that cadence, emailing a developer a list of changes once a quarter is fine — and often cheaper than a platform subscription.

The middle path: Squarespace-first, custom-later

Plenty of businesses start on Squarespace and move to custom once they've validated the business. This is a reasonable strategy — you avoid the up-front $1,500 until you know you're going to keep operating. The catch: the move itself takes work. Content needs to be ported, redirects set up, SEO preserved. Budget a few hundred dollars for that transition.

If you already know you're committed and you have $1,500, skipping Squarespace entirely saves you the transition headache.

What we'd recommend

If you're validating whether the business is real: start on Squarespace Personal ($16/mo), keep it scrappy, don't over-invest. Move to custom when you hit the revenue threshold that makes $1,500 an easy decision.

If you're already past that threshold — you have real revenue, real customers, and you intend to be in business in 2029 — skip Squarespace and build the custom site now. The year-five math favors it, the speed and SEO favor it, and you won't be paying Squarespace $276 a year forever.

Frequently asked questions

Is Squarespace cheaper than a custom website?
In year one, yes. Squarespace Business plan runs about $276 a year versus $1,500 for a one-time custom build. But starting in year two, a custom site on Cloudflare or similar hosting can cost under $50 a year to run. By year five, the custom site is often hundreds of dollars cheaper overall.
Do I own my Squarespace website?
You own your content and your domain, but not the underlying site. If you stop paying, the site goes offline. You cannot export a working Squarespace site to host elsewhere. A custom website is fully yours — the code, the design, and the hosting choice.
How fast is a Squarespace site compared to a custom site?
Squarespace sites typically score 50–75 on Google PageSpeed mobile. A well-built custom site on Cloudflare regularly scores 90+. Site speed affects both SEO rankings and conversion rate — every second of load time costs roughly 7 percent of conversions.
Can I customize a Squarespace template the way I want?
You can customize colors, fonts, and layouts within the template's rails. You cannot change fundamental structure, add custom interactions, or optimize code without paying a Squarespace developer, which offsets the DIY savings quickly.
What are the hidden costs of Squarespace?
The plan fee is only the start. Add domain renewal ($20–$25/year), email hosting ($6/user/month through Google Workspace or similar), premium templates or third-party integrations, and the cost of a Squarespace designer if you want customization. Typical year-one Squarespace total: $450–$900 once you add these up.
How much does a custom website cost per year after launch?
If built on modern static hosting like Cloudflare Pages or Workers, the ongoing cost is often under $50 per year — just the domain renewal. No monthly platform fee. You do give up easy drag-and-drop editing; edits typically go through the developer or a separate CMS.
