The Quick Answer: Website Costs in 2026 If you just want the numbers, here they are: • DIY website builder (Wix, Squarespace, WordPress.com): $0 - $500/year • Freelance web designer: $2,000 - $10,000 • Web design agency: $10,000 - $50,000+ • Flat-fee custom design (like Sitebolt): $1,500 But those numbers don't mean anything without context. A $300 Squarespace site and a $30,000 agency build serve completely different purposes. Let's break down each option so you can figure out what actually makes sense for your business. Option 1: DIY Website Builders ($0 - $500/year) Platforms like Wix, Squarespace, WordPress.com, and Shopify let you drag-and-drop your way to a website without writing a line of code. On paper, this sounds like a no-brainer for a small business. What you're actually paying • Platform subscription: $16 - $45/month ($192 - $540/year) • Custom domain: $10 - $20/year • Premium template (optional): $50 - $200 one-time • Premium plugins or apps: $0 - $50/month Total first-year cost: $200 - $800 The tradeoffs The dollar cost is low. The time cost is not. Most business owners underestimate how long it takes to build a site that doesn't look like every other template out there. We're talking 40-80+ hours of your time selecting templates, customizing layouts, writing copy, sourcing images, and troubleshooting mobile display issues. If your time is worth $50/hour, that "free" website just cost you $2,000 - $4,000 in opportunity cost. And the result still looks like a template -- because it is one. Best for: Side projects, personal sites, or businesses with near-zero budget and plenty of spare time. Option 2: Hiring a Freelancer ($2,000 - $10,000) A freelance web designer will build you a custom (or semi-custom) site. Quality and pricing vary wildly depending on their experience, location, and how much custom work is involved. What you're actually paying • Design and development: $2,000 - $10,000 • Hosting (separate): $5 - $30/month • Domain: $10 - $20/year • Revisions beyond scope: $50 - $150/hour Total first-year cost: $2,100 - $10,500+ The tradeoffs The quality range is massive. A $2,000 freelancer might give you something barely better than a template. A $10,000 freelancer might deliver something genuinely impressive. The challenge is that you often don't know what you're getting until the project is well underway. Timeline is another factor. Freelancers juggle multiple clients. A project quoted at "4-6 weeks" regularly stretches to 2-3 months. And if your freelancer ghosts you mid-project (it happens more than you'd think), you're starting over from scratch. Scope creep is the silent budget killer here. That "one small change" turns into a $500 invoice because it required restructuring the mobile layout. Always get a detailed scope of work in writing before you start. Best for: Businesses that need something custom, have a moderate budget, and are comfortable managing the project themselves. Option 3: Web Design Agency ($10,000 - $50,000+) Agencies bring the full production: strategy, branding, UX design, development, copywriting, QA testing, and ongoing support. You're paying for a team, not a person. What you're actually paying • Discovery and strategy: $2,000 - $5,000 • Design and development: $8,000 - $40,000 • Content/copywriting: $1,000 - $5,000 • Monthly maintenance retainer: $500 - $2,000/month • Hosting (managed): $50 - $200/month Total first-year cost: $15,000 - $75,000+ The tradeoffs If you're a funded startup, an e-commerce brand doing $1M+/year, or a company with genuinely complex technical needs, an agency might be the right call. They have the resources to handle large-scale projects with multiple integrations, custom functionality, and ongoing optimization. But for a local plumber, a dental practice, a med spa, or a landscaping company? You're massively overpaying. An agency's overhead -- office space, project managers, account executives, multiple designers and developers -- gets baked into your invoice. You're not paying $25,000 for a $25,000 website. You're paying $25,000 for a $5,000 website built by a team with $20,000 in overhead. Best for: Established businesses with complex requirements, large budgets, and the need for ongoing strategic support. Not a fortune, but not zero either. Anyone who tells you a website is a one-time expense is leaving out important details. How to Decide What's Right for Your Business Skip the analysis paralysis. Ask yourself three questions: 1 What's your budget? If it's under $500, you're going DIY. If it's $1,500 - $5,000, you're in freelancer or flat-fee territory. If it's $10K+, you can consider an agency. 2 How much time do you have? If you have 60+ hours to spare, DIY works. If you have a business to run, pay someone. 3 How complex is your project? A 5-page site for a local business is a fundamentally different project than a 50-page site with user accounts, payment processing, and third-party integrations. Match the tool to the job.
