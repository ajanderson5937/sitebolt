Website Redesign Cost in 2026: What You'll Actually Pay

April 23, 2026 · 9 min read

The honest answer is "anywhere from $500 to $50,000." Here's the honest breakdown of what each price tier actually buys you — and how to know which one you need.

Website redesign pricing is the most confusing category in small-business buying. The same five-page site for a plumber can be quoted at $600, $5,000, or $25,000 by three different vendors in the same week — and all three are being truthful about their pricing for what they do.

The reason: the word "redesign" covers wildly different things. It can mean "change the colors and swap some photos" (a Fiverr gig) or "rebuild the information architecture from discovery through launch with a team of six" (an agency engagement). Same two words, very different invoices.

Here's what each pricing tier really includes in 2026, and how to figure out which one actually fits your business.

What the cheap options actually deliver

Fiverr / offshore freelancers ($200–$800)

You can get a Fiverr seller to redo your site for $300. Here's what you'll actually receive: a stock theme installed on WordPress or Wix, your logo swapped in, your photos uploaded, and your text pasted into the template's existing blocks. Most sellers outsource to a team, so the person you talk to isn't the person building.

When this works: you already have a decent site structure, you just want it freshened up, and you'll do your own copywriting and QA. For a side project or a very early-stage business, this is fine.

When it doesn't: anything beyond the template's default patterns. Custom layouts, bespoke calculators, real SEO, fast load times — not happening at this price. The rework cost when you realize the site needs to be rebuilt usually exceeds what you saved.

US/Canada freelancers ($1,500–$5,000)

This is the middle of the market. You work directly with one designer-developer who builds on Squarespace, Webflow, WordPress, or occasionally custom code. You get more attention, better judgment, and a site that doesn't look cookie-cutter.

The variance is huge. A great freelancer at $3,000 will out-deliver a mediocre $15,000 agency. The trick is finding a great freelancer, which is mostly luck — check three references and look at three live sites they've built in the last year.

What the expensive options actually deliver

Small studios ($5,000–$15,000)

Small studios — typically 2 to 5 people, often design-led — give you more deliberation on the strategy side. You'll get a discovery call or two, a moodboard phase, probably a few rounds of design iteration, and a site that's been thought about harder than a freelancer would.

Worth it when: your brand identity needs work as part of the redesign, you're launching a new positioning, or you have specific audience research you want reflected in the design.

Not worth it when: you already know what your business is, your logo and colors are set, and you just need a better-looking site. You'll pay the studio to have meetings you didn't need.

Mid-size agencies ($15,000–$35,000)

At this tier you're buying a team: account manager, strategist, designer, developer, QA. The process is formalized with kickoff calls, weekly status meetings, and defined revision rounds. The deliverable is high-quality, and the process around it is expensive.

Worth it when: you're doing more than a website — you're overhauling your brand, launching a new product, or the site is the centerpiece of a larger marketing push. You need the coordination a project manager provides.

Not worth it when: you just want a nice website for a service business. You're paying for process you won't use.

Full-service agencies ($35,000+)

Reserved for companies where the website is a real business asset: e-commerce brands doing seven figures online, B2B SaaS with complex product pages, anyone whose site needs to do significant persuasive and conversion work. You're buying strategy, copywriting, custom illustration or photography, and ongoing optimization.

If you're reading a "redesign cost" article, you're almost certainly not in this tier. That's fine.

What drives the price up

Same five-page site, wildly different prices. Here's what moves the number:

  • Custom development — calculators, member areas, booking systems, integrations with a CRM. Each one adds days of dev time.
  • E-commerce — Shopify or a custom store adds $1,000–$5,000 minimum. Complex catalogs with variants, subscriptions, and tax handling add more.
  • Content migration — moving 100+ blog posts from one platform to another is labor-intensive and often underpriced.
  • Copywriting — real copywriting, not placeholder text, adds $1,500–$10,000 depending on depth.
  • Discovery phases — multi-week stakeholder workshops, competitive audits, user research. Standard at agencies, invisible on your final site but real on the invoice.
  • Revision rounds — every round past the first usually adds 15–25% to the build cost.
  • Stakeholder involvement — if 4 people on your side have to approve every decision, the project takes longer. Longer = more expensive.

What keeps the price down

Inverse list:

  • Fixed scope. "Seven pages, standard components, one round of revisions" is way cheaper than "whatever we decide we need."
  • One decision-maker. One person approves, one person pays. No committees.
  • You bring the copy. If you can write your own About page and service descriptions, you save thousands.
  • Standard platform. WordPress, Squarespace, Webflow, or hand-coded static. Custom platforms cost more to build and maintain.
  • No e-commerce. If you don't need to sell online through the site, don't build a store.

How to figure out what you actually need

Answer these four questions honestly:

  1. Is the website currently costing you business? If your existing site is so bad it's actively losing you leads, fix it now, cheaply, and move on. Don't spend $30k on the perfect site when a $1,500 site would stop the bleeding this month.
  2. How long will this site need to last? If you plan to redesign again in 2 years, a $1,500 flat-fee site is the right call. If this needs to carry your brand for 5+ years and you're in a design-forward industry, pay for quality up front.
  3. Who's going to update it? If you update weekly, you need a CMS you can actually use — Squarespace, Webflow, or WordPress. If you update quarterly, a hand-coded static site with a developer on call is fine and faster.
  4. What's the revenue impact of a better site? A law firm where each lead is worth $5k can justify a $25k site. A coffee shop where the site mostly serves hours and directions probably cannot.

How much does a website redesign cost in 2026?
A small-business redesign in 2026 typically runs $1,500–$8,000 for a small shop or freelancer, $8,000–$25,000 for a mid-size agency, and $30,000+ for a full-service agency with branding, copy, and strategy included. Is it cheaper to redesign my website or start from scratch?
Almost always cheaper to start from scratch if you're moving platforms, if your current site is more than three years old, or if the existing code is a mess. Trying to rework a legacy WordPress or Wix setup often costs 60–80 percent of a fresh build for a worse result.
How long does a website redesign take?
Freelancer: 3–6 weeks. Small studio: 4–8 weeks. Mid-size agency: 2–4 months. Full-service agency with discovery and strategy: 3–6 months. Productized flat-fee services like Sitebolt deliver in 2 weeks because scope is pre-defined.
What makes a website redesign expensive?
Three things: scope, people, and process. Scope grows when you add custom functionality, e-commerce, integrations, or content migration. People cost more at agencies with account managers, strategists, and designers layered in. Process adds weeks — discovery phases, revision rounds, stakeholder meetings — each billable.
Can I redesign my website myself?
Yes — Squarespace, Wix, and Shopify are built for this. Expect to spend 20–60 hours of your own time, $200–$1,000 on platform fees and templates, and end up with something functional but template-bound. Most owner-built redesigns still look like everyone else's.
