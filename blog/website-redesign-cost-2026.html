Website Redesign Cost in 2026: What You'll Actually Pay

April 24, 2026 · 8 min read

Your website is starting to look dated. Maybe it was built in 2019 on a theme you're tired of. Maybe your branding has evolved and the site hasn't. Maybe mobile traffic is now 70% of your visitors and your site still feels like a 2015 desktop design. Whatever the reason -- you're ready for a redesign.

The first question is almost always the same: what does a website redesign actually cost in 2026? The answer ranges from a few hundred dollars to over $100,000, and the difference comes down to scope, team size, and whether you're rebuilding or refreshing. Here's the honest breakdown.

  • Full visual redesign (same content, new design): $2,000 - $8,000
  • Complete rebuild (new design + new structure + new CMS): $5,000 - $25,000
  • Enterprise redesign (large site, multiple stakeholders, migrations): $25,000 - $150,000+

What Drives the Cost of a Redesign

Price varies wildly because "redesign" means different things to different people. The four factors that actually determine what you'll pay:

1. Number of pages

A 5-page small business site is a completely different project than a 50-page corporate site with blog, portfolio, case studies, and team bios. Most agencies price per template, not per page, but the more unique layouts required, the higher the cost.

2. Are you keeping the content or rewriting it?

Content rewriting is where agencies quietly add $2,000-$10,000 to the invoice. If you can reuse your existing copy (or you're writing the new copy yourself), your redesign cost drops considerably. If you need them to rewrite everything, expect to pay for professional copywriting.

3. Platform migration

Moving from WordPress to Webflow, or from Squarespace to a custom build, or from Shopify's old theme to a new one -- migrations take time. Migrating content, redirecting old URLs, and preserving SEO rankings are their own line items.

4. Who's doing the work

An agency costs more than a freelancer, and a freelancer costs more than a flat-fee service. You're paying for overhead, account management, and meeting time on every invoice. More people = bigger invoice, not always better work.

Redesign Cost by Approach

DIY redesign on Squarespace or Wix ($0 - $500)

If your current site is already on Squarespace, Wix, or WordPress.com, you can swap templates for free and redo your site yourself. Budget 20-60 hours of your time. Result: probably better than before, but still looks like a template.

Best for: Businesses that can afford the time but not the money.

Freelance redesign ($2,000 - $8,000)

A freelance designer can do a full refresh or rebuild for $2K-$8K depending on experience and location. You'll work directly with one person, timelines are flexible, and quality varies wildly.

Best for: Small businesses with moderate budgets that want something custom but can't afford agency pricing.

Watch out for: Scope creep, mid-project disappearing acts, and "out of scope" surcharges.

Agency redesign ($10,000 - $50,000+)

A full agency redesign includes discovery, stakeholder interviews, UX research, wireframes, visual design, development, QA, and launch. For a 10-20 page small business site, this is usually 3-6 months and $15K-$40K.

Best for: Funded startups, established brands, or companies with complex integration needs.

Watch out for: Discovery fatigue, endless meetings, and paying for process you didn't need.

Flat-fee redesign ($1,500 - $3,500)

Services like Sitebolt rebuild a small business site from scratch for a flat fee. You fill out an intake form, they design and develop, you launch in 14 days. No hourly billing, no scope games.

Best for: Small businesses that want a professional, custom site without the agency price tag.

Hidden Costs Nobody Talks About

The sticker price is almost never the real price. Before you sign a redesign contract, ask about:

  • Content migration fees. Moving 20 blog posts from old CMS to new? That's often $500-$2,000 extra.
  • Redirect mapping. If your URLs change, you need 301 redirects to preserve SEO. Agencies charge for this.
  • Image optimization. If your old site has 400 full-resolution JPEGs, someone has to compress and reformat them.
  • Stock photography. Good stock images cost $10-$50 each. A 10-page site might need 15-30 of them.
  • Third-party integrations. Reconnecting email marketing, CRM, booking systems -- all billable time.
  • Training. If the new site uses a CMS, someone has to train your team to use it.
  • Post-launch fixes. Every site has quirks after launch. Does your contract cover 30 days of bug fixes?

Signs You Need a Redesign (Not Just a Refresh)

Not every tired-looking site needs a full redesign. Sometimes a minor refresh -- new colors, updated photos, better copy -- is enough. A full redesign is warranted when:

  • Mobile traffic is 50%+ and your site doesn't work well on phones
  • Your site is slower than 3 seconds to load
  • Your branding has changed meaningfully
  • You've added or dropped major services that aren't reflected
  • Your competitors have obviously better sites
  • You're embarrassed to send people to your current site

If three or more apply, you need a redesign, not a refresh.

How to Avoid Overpaying

Redesign overspending almost always comes from one of four mistakes:

  1. Confusing scope. Get a detailed, written scope of work before you agree to pricing. Anything not listed will become a change order.
  2. Paying for process. You don't need a 6-week discovery phase for a 10-page small business site. Skip the deliverable-heavy agency process and pick someone who just builds.
  3. Custom development that wasn't needed. If your site doesn't have custom features, you don't need a custom development platform. HTML and Cloudflare will serve 95% of small business sites perfectly.
  4. Paying hourly. Hourly billing creates perverse incentives. Every "quick fix" somehow takes 4 hours. Flat-fee pricing solves this.

How long does a website redesign take?

Should I redesign my website or just refresh it?

Refresh if you only need new colors, fonts, or a better hero image -- usually $500-$2,000 and a few weeks. Redesign if your mobile experience is broken, your site loads slowly, your branding has changed, or your structure no longer fits your business. A full redesign costs $2,000-$25,000 depending on scope.

Will a website redesign hurt my SEO rankings?

It can, if URLs change without 301 redirects, if page structure changes drastically, or if Core Web Vitals get worse after launch. A well-planned redesign should preserve or improve rankings. Make sure your redesigner maps old URLs to new ones, preserves title tags and meta descriptions, and tests page speed before launch.

Do I need to rewrite my website content during a redesign?

Not necessarily. If your existing copy is working, reuse it. If it's outdated, unclear, or missing sections, rewrite those parts -- you don't have to rewrite the entire site. Content rewriting adds $2,000-$10,000 to an agency redesign, so decide upfront what's staying and what's changing.
