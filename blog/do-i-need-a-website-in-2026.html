Do I Need a Website in 2026? The Honest Answer

April 23, 2026 · 7 min read

Yes — for almost every real business. Not because the internet says so, but because the customers you want are checking, and the ones who don't find a site assume you're not for them.

There's a recurring conversation happening with small business owners in 2026: "I have Instagram, I have Google Business, I have 4.8 stars on Yelp. Do I really need an actual website?"

The answer is yes for roughly 95% of small businesses, and the reason has changed since 2019. A website in 2026 isn't primarily about discovery anymore — Google Business and social do that work. It's about conversion and trust. The people who already know you exist and are deciding whether to give you money are the ones looking at your website.

What a website actually does in 2026

The job of a small business website has shifted. Here's what it does now:

  • Proves you're a real business. Anyone can make a Facebook page in 10 minutes. A website signals operational seriousness.
  • Answers "will this be right for me?" Services, scope, prices, area served, examples of work. People need specifics before they reach out.
  • Converts referrals. The person a friend told about you is going to Google you. That's the moment a website matters most.
  • Shows up for long-tail searches. "plumber near me open sunday," "family law attorney cedar park," "best pediatric dentist spanish speaking." Google Business won't rank for these. A site with real content will.
  • Owns your story. Instagram can throttle you, Yelp can hide reviews, Facebook can change the algorithm. Your website is the one place you control.

The real cost of not having one

Every small business owner underestimates this, so run the math:

Assume you run a service business doing $100k/year. That's roughly $8,300/month in revenue. Industry research consistently finds that somewhere between 70% and 85% of consumers look up a local business online before contacting them. Of that group, a meaningful fraction — studies suggest 30–45% — won't contact a business that doesn't have a functioning website.

Conservatively: 30% of buyers check for a site, 30% of those abandon if there isn't one. That's 9% of potential customers lost to "I can't find them online." For an $8,300/month business, that's $750/month in lost revenue, every month, forever.

A $1,500 custom website pays back in two months and keeps paying forever. A $300 Wix site pays back in twelve days.

The framing that matters: a website isn't a marketing expense. It's the floor under your whole sales process. Without it, people who decided to hire you before visiting the site change their mind at the site.

What doesn't count as having a website

Several things often get counted as "having a website" that don't actually do the job:

A Google Business Profile

GBP is essential — you absolutely should have one, fully filled out, with photos and regular posts. But it's a listing, not a site. You can't tell your story. You can't showcase custom work beyond photos. You can't add pages, blog, or capture leads. It's the Yellow Pages, not your storefront.

A Facebook or Instagram page

Social profiles discover you but don't sell for you. Product searches don't lead there. SEO doesn't live there. Many serious customers — especially over 40 — still mentally distinguish "real business with a website" from "Facebook-only operation" and treat the second as less serious.

A Linktree or Beacons page

Fine as a temporary stopgap, terrible as a real site. It looks like a side hustle even if you aren't one. A Linktree tells sophisticated buyers you haven't invested in the business.

A Yelp or Thumbtack profile

You don't own it. The review platform can change policies tomorrow. You can't rank for anything except their directory page. And you're paying them to send you leads they could redirect to competitors next week.

An old website from 2016 that still technically loads

This is the biggest trap. A genuinely bad website is often worse than no website. If your existing site is slow, mobile-broken, or visually stuck in 2016, visitors will form an opinion of your business based on it. That opinion won't be good.

The rare cases where you can skip it

A few businesses genuinely don't need a website in 2026:

  1. Appointment-only by referral practices. A high-end therapist, a specialty consultant, a boutique surgeon who takes patients only through referral from other doctors. These operate on trust chains, not searches.
  2. Cash-only street-level retail. A hot dog cart, a farmer's market vendor, a taco truck. Google Business + Instagram does the whole job.
  3. Temporary pop-ups and tests. Running a pop-up for three weekends to validate demand? Skip the site, do Instagram only, come back to the website question if the business survives.
  4. Strictly B2B relationship businesses that never take inbound leads. A commercial landscaper who only services managed property portfolios under existing contracts. No cold inbound to serve.

If you're not one of those four cases, you need a website.

What counts as a "good enough" website in 2026

Here's the floor. Your site needs, at minimum:

  1. A clear headline above the fold — what you do and who you do it for, in plain English.
  2. Your service area or location — the most common question "does this business serve my area" needs to be answered in three seconds.
  3. Basic service descriptions or a menu of offerings — what they can actually buy.
  4. Pricing signals — either real prices or a clear "starting at" or "projects from $X." Not "contact for pricing" if you can avoid it; people bounce.
  5. One proof point — a review quote, a photo of work, a recognizable client logo, a portfolio sample. Something that says "these people are real."
  6. Obvious contact method — phone, email, or form. On every page.
  7. Mobile-responsive — 70%+ of your traffic is on mobile.
  8. Fast — under 3 seconds to interactive on mobile. Squarespace default themes often fail this; custom static sites pass easily.

That's the floor. Most small businesses don't need more than that to win their local market.

What the delay is actually about

Most owners who haven't built a site yet aren't being cheap. Three real reasons it keeps getting pushed:

  1. It feels like it'll take forever and be painful. Historically, it did. Six-week freelancer engagements, three rounds of revisions, stakeholder meetings that never quite end. In 2026 this is solved — productized flat-fee services deliver in 2 weeks without a single call.
  2. Writing the content feels daunting. Any decent builder gives you a short intake form and drafts your copy for you. You edit, you don't write from scratch.
  3. You don't want to pick the wrong platform. Fine — get a custom site that doesn't lock you in. If you ever want to move, the code is yours.

None of these are reasons to keep losing $750/month. They're reasons to pick a service that handles the friction for you.

The cheapest way forward

If the budget is under $500: buy a domain, pick Wix or Squarespace's cheapest tier, spend a weekend on it, launch something minimum-viable. It won't be great, but it'll stop the bleeding.

If the budget is $1,500+: skip the hosted platforms, get a flat-fee custom build, be done in two weeks. You'll have a site that ranks, loads fast, looks professional, and has no monthly fee eating into your margins.

The worst option is the one most people pick: wait another six months to "figure it out." That six months costs more than the site.

Frequently asked questions

Do I still need a website in 2026 if I have Instagram and Google Business?
Yes, for most small businesses. Instagram and Google Business Profile drive discovery but don't close. Roughly 60 percent of people who find you on social media check for a website before contacting you. If there isn't one, a meaningful share assume the business isn't active or serious and move to a competitor.
Can I just use a Facebook page or Linktree instead?
For a side project or very early-stage business, yes temporarily. For a real business, no. You don't own the platform, you can't rank on Google, and a Linktree screams side hustle to anyone evaluating whether to hire you for real work.
What's the minimum viable website in 2026?
One page that clearly states: what you do, who you do it for, where you operate, one proof point (photo, review, or example), and how to contact you. That's the floor. Anything less and you're not really in the game.
How much does it cost to have a website in 2026?
Anywhere from $20 a year for a DIY one-pager on a free host, to $1,500–$5,000 for a custom-built professional site, to $10,000+ for agency work. Most small businesses should budget $1,500–$3,000 for the initial build and under $500 per year ongoing.
What happens if I keep delaying?
The math is straightforward: every month without a website, a percentage of people who would have contacted you go to a competitor instead. For a service business doing $100k/year, that's often $1,000–$5,000 per month in lost revenue. The cost of a website pays back in weeks for most businesses.
Is it too late to start if I've been in business for years without one?
No. Existing businesses who finally get online often see larger lifts than new ones because they already have word-of-mouth flow. Referrals who would otherwise Google you and find nothing now find a real site and convert at a higher rate.
